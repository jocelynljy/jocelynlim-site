What a Newsroom Taught Me About Hosting a Room

By Jocelyn Lim (Jocelyn Lim Jin Yan) ·June 2026 ·3 min read

My first real job wasn't on a stage with a microphone. It was in a newsroom.

As a student journalist at Singapore Press Holdings, my whole day was walking up to people I'd never met — founders, business owners, strangers with interesting lives — and getting them to tell me something true. I was nervous every single time. I also loved it more than anything I'd done. It took me years to realise that job quietly taught me how to host.

Hosting is mostly listening

People assume an emcee's job is talking. It isn't. The best hosting, like the best interviewing, is mostly listening — reading the room, noticing who's gone quiet, catching the exact moment the energy dips so you can lift it. In the newsroom I learned that if you genuinely listen, people give you far more than they planned to. A room is the same. It can feel the difference between someone performing at it and someone paying attention to it.

Prepare obsessively, then let it go

Before every interview I over-prepared — background, questions, the order I'd ask them. And then, in the room, I'd throw half of it away and follow the person in front of me. Hosting works exactly like that. I'll build a run sheet down to the minute, then stay loose enough to ride whatever the room actually gives me. Preparation isn't a script; it's what lets you improvise without fear.

The host is not the star

A journalist serves the story, not their own ego. A host serves the room. My job isn't to be the most memorable person on stage — it's to make everyone else feel like the night was theirs. When I get that right, people don't leave saying "great emcee." They leave saying "what a great night." That's the whole point.

One good question changes everything

The thing from journalism I use most: the right question, at the right moment, can change the entire temperature of a room. It can turn a flat panel into a real conversation, or a stiff crowd into people who are suddenly leaning in. Hosting just gave me a live, room-sized version of the same craft.

So when people ask how someone "non-traditional" ended up holding microphones, this is the honest answer: I've been doing a version of it since I was a student with a notebook, getting strangers to open up. The stage just made it louder.
